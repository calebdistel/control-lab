## Hook

Picture this: you tell your friend, "make me a sandwich." They walk into the kitchen and come back two minutes later with a sandwich. Easy.

Now imagine your friend is a robot. You say "make me a sandwich." The robot stands there. You sigh, and try again: "Pick up the bread." The robot picks up the entire loaf — wrapper and all — and hands it to you.

Programming a robot is exactly this conversation. Every instruction you'd skip when talking to a human, you have to say out loud to the machine. This lesson is about learning to think in that language.

## Core idea

> A program is a sequence of instructions, executed in order, by something that does exactly what you say — no more, no less.

That last part is the trap. A computer (or a robot) will do precisely what your program tells it to. If your program is wrong in a subtle way, the robot will be subtly wrong, confidently, fifty times a second.

## Walk-through

Let's write a "program" in plain English first. The task: get a robot arm from resting at 0° to holding a game piece at 90°.

A first attempt:

> 1. Move the arm to 90°.

That's not a program — that's a goal. A program would look more like:

> 1. Read the current arm angle from the sensor.
> 2. If the angle is less than 90°, run the motor up.
> 3. If the angle is more than 90°, run the motor down.
> 4. If the angle is close enough to 90°, stop the motor.
> 5. Go back to step 1.

Notice three things:

- **Every step is something the robot can actually do.** "Move to 90°" isn't one of those things — but "run the motor" is.
- **There's a loop.** The robot doesn't do this once. It does it ~50 times per second, forever, while the robot is on.
- **There's a decision.** "If… then…" is how a program responds to the world.

This five-line program is, almost literally, your first controller. We'll come back to it many times.

## Try it yourself

Trace this program **by hand** — don't run it, just write down what each step produces:

```
angle = 0
Step A:  angle = angle + 30
Step B:  angle = angle + 30
Step C:  angle = angle - 10
Step D:  angle = angle + 30
```

What is `angle` after step D? Write it down before checking.

> Answer: 80. If you got 80, you just executed a program in your head. That's all programming is, just faster.

## What just happened

- A program is a sequence of instructions executed in order.
- Computers do exactly what you say — including your mistakes.
- Robot programs almost always loop, reading sensors and deciding what to do.
- "Move the arm to 90°" is a goal; "run the motor while angle < 90°" is a program.

## Common confusions

**"This seems too simple."** It is, on purpose. The reason students struggle later isn't that programs get conceptually complicated — it's that they get *long*. The skill we're building right now is the discipline of being explicit. Build it on something easy.

**"Won't the robot figure out what I mean?"** No. This is the single most important thing to internalize. A robot has no common sense. Every senior on 498 has, at some point, lost an hour debugging because they assumed the robot knew something it didn't.
